Historical Bushfire Trends in Australia

Every year Australia experiences a large amount of Bushfires with varying intensity, being known as a dry and arid land these can have devastating effects. This visualisation will explore how through recent years the frequency,

location and temperature of these bush fires have evolved, it will also explore potential explanatory conditions that may influence these bushfires such as the changing temperature, possible due to climate change.

Spread of Fire

From the map, it can be observed that majority of the fires occur within a few hundred kilometres of the coast, this is expected as this is where majority of the flora is located, being susceptible to the harsh environmental conditions. Looking closer, a large majority of the data points lie along the top of the country potentially due to the hotter conditions being located to the equator. It is worth noting that the middle portion of Australia is mostly desert conditions, so despite the warmer conditions is unable to sustain bushfires, thus the lack in the middle of the country.


Annual Temperature Evolution

Inspecting the map it reveals that along the south-east and southern borders the average maximum temperature is significantly lower than at points closer to the equator. This information supports the bushfire distribution map where a larger frequency is recorded along the top of Australia. Moving through time on this map, the weather stations record an increase in the average annual temperature suggesting a nation wide increase, which may be a cause in an increase of bushfires.



Increase in Fires

From the graph it suggests that there is a trend of increasing bushfires in Australia on a yearly basis, this could be due to warmer conditions, drier conditions or more people setting fires. An important consideration to be made is that recording mechanisms may have improved over time leading to more results being captured than have been in the past.

Distribution by State

As expected more northern states such as NSW and WA recorded the highest amount of bushfires in the whole time period, with southern states as TAS and SA demonstrating a low count. Interestingly VIC had the third highest recorded count of fires over the whole period.


Note: The Northern Territory was not included in the dataset


Change in Ingnition Cause

Comparing the two charts, there has been a noticeable increase in the amount of fires that have been caused by accidental or incendiary means, with a slight uptick in those generated by natural means. This suggests that the cause for this increase of bushfires demonstrated by the line chart may not be all environmentally sourced but due to nefarious or neglectful acts.
