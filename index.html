Historical Bushfire Trends in Australia

Every year Australia experiences a large amount of Bushfires with varying intensity, being known as a dry and arid land these can have devastating effects. This visualisation will explore how through recent years the frequency,

location and temperature of these bush fires have evolved, it will also explore potential explanatory conditions that may influence these bushfires such as the changing temperature, possible due to climate change.

Spread of Fire

  • Majority of fires occur close to the coastline where flora is denser and more susceptible to the drier conditions to spark a fire.
  • Fires are more dense along the north of the country, where its closer to the equator and experiences warmer conditions.
  • The stronger desert conditions in the centre lack the vegetation to support bush fires, along with low population in these areas may not support as in depth recording techniques.
  • Most intense fires by temperature occurred in the lower parts of Australia

Annual Temperature Evolution

  • South-east and southern regions historically record among the lowest temperatures, due to geographic location from the equator.
  • As the year progresses to the current day, the average maximum temperature is increasing nation wide, may induce more bushfires.
  • The distribution and frequency of the bush fires aligns with the temperature map, with a higher frequency of bush fires in regions of severe heat.

Increase in Fires

  • Yearly fire frequency trend is increasing.
  • This increase could be due to warmer conditions as suggested by temperature map.
  • Increase may come from more sophisticated recording techniques being developed and used, so old data may be unreliable.

Distribution by State

  • Northern and Central areas such as NSW and WA recorded a high frequency of fires.
  • Southern states such as TAS and SA demonstrated a low recorded amount of fires.
  • Despites its southern location VIC had the third highest frequency over the whole time period.


Note: The Northern Territory was not included in the dataset

Change in Ingnition Cause

  • Increase in the proportion of ignitions by accidental means.
  • Decrease in proportion of ignition by incendiary.
  • Increase in proportion by natural means, source may due to harsher weather conditions, an outcome of climate change.
